Decoding Functional Networks for Visual Categories via Graph Neural Networks

Key Contributions

We introduce an interpretable framework for decoding visual categories from stimulus-driven functional connectivity in human cortex.

  • Multimodal labeling pipeline: COCO instance masks and captions are fused to derive reliable super-category assignments for sports, food, and vehicle stimuli.
  • Signed connectivity modeling: a signed GNN learns from both positive and negative functional interactions instead of flattening connectivity matrices into vectors.
  • Global and class-specific explainability: a learned sparse edge mask reveals a shared connectivity backbone, and Gradient × Input saliency highlights category-selective subnetworks.
  • Biologically meaningful results: the learned networks recover ventral, dorsal, and limbic patterns consistent with known category-selective visual systems.

Abstract

Understanding how large-scale brain networks represent visual categories is fundamental to linking perception and cortical organization. Using high-resolution 7T fMRI from the Natural Scenes Dataset, we construct parcel-level functional graphs and train a signed Graph Neural Network that models both positive and negative interactions, with interpretability enabled by a sparse edge mask and class-specific saliency.

The model accurately decodes stimulus categories including sports, food, and vehicles, and reveals reproducible, biologically meaningful subnetworks along the ventral and dorsal visual pathways. This framework bridges machine learning and neuroscience by extending voxel-level category selectivity to a connectivity-based representation of visual processing.

Background

fMRI measures brain activity indirectly through the BOLD signal. When different brain regions fluctuate together, they are functionally connected, and these interactions can be modeled as networks. Most decoding approaches still focus on voxels, ROIs, or vectorized connectivity features, which weakens network-level interpretation.

Our goal is to model functional brain networks directly, decode visual categories, identify which regions communicate, and reveal category-selective subnetworks under naturalistic viewing.

Motivation

Visual processing relies on distributed interactions between brain regions rather than only local responses. Prior graph-learning work on fMRI has focused mainly on resting-state or clinical settings, while stimulus-driven, signed connectivity remains less explored.

We therefore ask whether an explainable signed GNN can decode visual categories from naturalistic fMRI and reveal the connectivity structure that supports each decision.

Method

We use the Natural Scenes Dataset (NSD), a large-scale 7T stimulus-driven fMRI resource with eight participants and up to about 10,000 natural images per subject. Images are drawn from COCO and each image is shown for 4 seconds during a continuous recognition task.

After preprocessing and HCP-MMP1 parcellation, parcel-wise time series are converted into functional connectivity matrices. Each matrix is split into positive and negative channels and processed by a signed GNN with a learned sparse edge mask. Explanations are obtained through the global edge mask and category-specific Gradient × Input saliency.

Label Fusion and Connectivity Construction

We define three super-categories: sports, food, and vehicle. For each NSD image, we combine spatial evidence from COCO instance masks with semantic evidence from captions. The fused score determines how strongly each image supports a category and guides soft label realization.

Each graph sample is built from parcel-wise fMRI time series grouped into blocks, concatenated across time, and converted into a Pearson correlation matrix. Positive and negative correlations are then separated into signed adjacency channels for graph learning.

Explainability

The training objective combines cross-entropy with mask regularization, encouraging a sparse and interpretable connectivity backbone. The learned edge mask provides a global, category-agnostic view of important connections across the dataset.

To recover category-specific relevance, we compute Gradient × Input saliency maps for sports, food, and vehicle. Together, these two views provide both stable global structure and fine-grained category-selective subnetworks.

Experiments and Results

Across subjects, the signed GNN learns meaningful and separable connectivity representations. In t-SNE space, clusters for sports, food, and vehicle emerge progressively during training, indicating that the model disentangles category-selective connectivity structure.

On the right hemisphere, the model reaches mean accuracy of 0.78 and mean macro-AP of 0.88. On the left hemisphere, performance is similarly strong, with accuracy of 0.80 and macro-AP of 0.91. Food achieves the highest average precision, followed by sports and vehicle.

Hemisphere Accuracy Sports AP Food AP Vehicle AP Macro-AP
RH 0.78 0.88 0.92 0.84 0.88
LH 0.80 0.90 0.94 0.88 0.91

Compared with a GraphCL-style contrastive baseline, the supervised signed GNN performs substantially better. The baseline reaches accuracy 0.637 and macro-AP 0.698, while our model reaches 0.780 and 0.880, respectively.

Method Accuracy Sports AP Food AP Vehicle AP Macro-AP
GraphCL baseline 0.637 0.728 0.752 0.613 0.698
Signed GNN (ours) 0.780 0.880 0.920 0.840 0.880

Relevance maps reveal category-selective subnetworks. Food emphasizes ventral visual and limbic-orbitofrontal regions, sports emphasizes dorsal visuomotor and parietal regions, and vehicle emphasizes lateral occipital and ventral visual regions.

Conclusion

Visual categories are encoded in distributed functional networks. The proposed signed GNN decodes category information from stimulus-driven connectivity while preserving the structure of positive and negative edges. By combining a learned edge mask with Gradient × Input saliency, the framework provides both accurate decoding and interpretable explanations of category-selective brain networks.
